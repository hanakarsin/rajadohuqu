Meet twin doctors encouraging people to get COVID-19 vaccine

Publish date: 2024-04-03

- The twins who are professional doctors urged people to go for the COVID-19 vaccine

- Dr Elana and Dr Delana took their vaccines recently

- The two have been using their platforms to sensitise people about the jab

When news broke that there was a vaccine to prevent people against the noble coronavirus, many people were not sure about its effectiveness.

While some went for it and have been hopeful that it works for the best, many are still adamant about taking the same, fearing various alleged side effects".

Well, the adamancy let two twin sisters to take it upon themselves to sensitise people about the COVID-19 vaccines and actually encouraging people to take them.

TUKO.co.ke has learnt Dr. Elana McDonald and her twin sister Dr. Delana Wardlaw recently took their vaccines and thought it wise to encourage more people to do the same.

The twins, who are doctors recently joined many healthcare workers in receiving their first doses of the vaccine.

It was after receiving their doses when they came out and through their platform as medical professionals educated community members about health issues and address health disparities, including the now deadly COVID-19.

The doctor twins said they went for the vaccines after clinical data showed that it was safe and effective.

Dr. McDonald on her part said she did not experience any allergic reactions or side effects other than mild soreness in her upper arm where she was injected.

“We do know that with the vaccine – as with any vaccine – there is always the possibility of some side effects but what we have to look at is are the side effects just as bad as having the disease itself and the answer is no,” McDonald said.

The twins, who are Philadelphia-based physicians noted that some African Americans are afraid of getting the vaccine because they are concerned about the accelerated time frame in which the vaccines were developed.

Some, according to the twin doctors, are afraid because they do not trust the medical establishment.

According to NBC Philadelphia, many are yet to get the vaccines because of the distrust rising from the 1932-1972 Tuskegee syphilis experiment by the U.S. government, where hundreds of Black men who took part in the study were denied care and treatment for the disease long after a cure became available.

One of the twins, Dr. Wardlaw disclosed she is aware that some African Americans have expressed concern about being used as guinea pigs.

With that said, she wants community members to know that African American organisations, physicians, scientists and medical schools are actively involved in monitoring how the vaccines were developed and being rolled out to communities equitably.
