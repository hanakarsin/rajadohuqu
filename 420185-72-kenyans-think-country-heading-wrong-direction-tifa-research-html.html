A new poll has revealed over three-quarters of Kenyans believe the country is headed in the wrong direction under the Jubilee administration headed by President Uhuru Kenyatta.

The latest survey conducted by TIFA Research showed 72% of Kenyans felt the country was headed in the wrong direction.

According to the survey, Kenyans cited poor economy (54%), high cost of living (38%), unemployment (16%), political divisions (13%) and corruption (10%) as some of the biggest concerns.

"For Kenya as a whole, just over one in ten (12%) consider Kenya’s current direction as the “right” one, while nearly three-quarters (72%) say the country is moving in the wrong direction.There is even more agreement about the presence of economic challenges at the local level, with the top four mentions (unemployment, the cost-of-living, hunger/famine, and poverty) in total attracting a clear majority of all responses (64%)," the survey stated.

The COVID-19 pandemic (16%), which has ravaged the country killing over 3,800 people and infected over 198,000 others, was also cited as one of the problems facing the country.

The survey analysed economic, political, and social welfare public issues.

Uhuru's legacy

On the other hand, almost half of Kenyans (47%) considered infrastructure the most positive accomplishment for which President Uhuru Kenyatta would be remembered about.

Kenyans surveyed listed enhancing national unity through the handshake (10%), extending the electricity grid (4%), and providing financial support for needy students (2%) as other reasons.

"On the negative side, no particular failing stands out, but the three most frequent mentions are increased public debt, general economic hardship of Kenyans, and increased corruption (18%, 17%, and 11%, respectively," the survey added.

Kenyans blame Uhuru

In June 2019, a poll by Infrotrak revealed 48% of Kenyans felt the country was headed in the wrong direction under the Jubilee government.

The survey findings released by Infotrak CEO Angela Ambitho, showed 55% of Kenyans believed high cost of living was the main concern; 33% cited corruption as their top concern, and 18% felt unemployment was the biggest problem.

Some 26% of those interviewed felt Uhuru was solely responsible for steering the country in the wrong direction; 15% blamed all political leaders; 11% blamed both the president and his deputy William Ruto, and 9% took issue with the citizens.
