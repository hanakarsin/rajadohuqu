Opinion: Magufulis COVID-19 absurdity proves why Kenyans must take pride in Uhurus leadership

Publish date: 2024-07-30

PAY ATTENTION: Don't miss trending Kenyan news. Follow TUKO.co.ke on Twitter!

Even as the rest of the world scrambled to contain the spread of the COVID-19 pandemic early last year, Tanzania treated the deadly pandemic like a joke.

President John Magufuli farcically downplayed the severity of the pandemic, declaring his country will not impose curfews and lockdowns witnessed in other East African countries such as Kenya, Uganda and Rwanda.

In taking the strange stance, he found company in former Burundi president Pierre Nkurunziza, but citizens were jolted back to reality when he succumbed to the disease in June 2020, two months before he could step down.

Before his death, the strongman had declared that the pandemic is transmitted by air and God has “cleared the coronavirus from Burundi’s skies.”

On May 15, his government expelled representatives of the World Health Organisation (WHO) from the country, accusing them of manipulating the outbreak.

Magufuli similarly remained stubborn and even at one time blocked Kenyans from entering his country in protest over a government move to include Tanzanians in the list of foreigners liable for mandatory quarantine upon setting foot in the country.

Indeed, he had no time for the basic protocols outlined by health experts such as regular hand washing, wearing masks and social distancing, which were being strictly enforced by security agencies in Kenya.

While Kenya shut down schools and entertainment places, banned political rallies and capped the number of people allowed in places of worship, funerals and public service transport vehicles, Tanzania allowed normal social, economic and political activities to take place without hindrance even as global health experts and countries such as the US warned COVI-19 deaths were taking place unreported.

Magufuli actually encouraged mass congregation in churches and mosques as an antidote to the coronavirus.

He even publicly questioned the correctness of COVID-19 tests, claiming lab equipment manufactured in the West was faulty and hence provided false positive results.

This is despite reviews by the US Centres for Disease Control and Prevention finding the procedure to be accurate.

He had alleged that samples of a papaya fruit, quail, and a goat he had dispatched to the national library returned positive results.

Then in June, Magufuli declared Tanzania was coronavirus free, paving the way for huge political gatherings at campaigns ahead of the October General Election, which he won on the back of strong-arm tactics.

His firm position was that Tanzanians should continue with prayers as they held the key to getting cured.

“I want to thank Tanzanians of all faiths. We have been praying and fasting for God to save us from the pandemic that has afflicted our country and the world. But God has answered us. I believe, and I’m certain that many Tanzanians believe, that the corona disease has been eliminated by God,” he told amused worshippers during a Sunday service in Dodoma at the time.

When scientists recently made a major breakthrough by finding a vaccine, Magufuli wildly declared Tanzania will not embrace them since they did not want to be used as guinea pigs by western scientists.

“If the white man was able to come up with vaccinations, then vaccinations for HIV/Aids would have been brought, tuberculosis would be a thing of the past, vaccines for malaria and cancer would have been found,” he claimed.

Instead, he has been advocating that Tanzanians use herbal medicine - particularly inhaling steam from a mixture of traditional herbs, as a therapy against the virus.

The COVID-19 situation in Tanzania remains unknown since, unlike Kenya which releases daily data, the last time authorities in Dar issued theirs was on April 29, 2020 when there were 509 cases, with 21 deaths.

However, the lack of transparency regarding the COVID-19 situation is doing Tanzania more harm than good.

According to the US embassy, there has been a surge in the number of cases since January this year and that some health facilities could soon get overwhelmed.

“The U.S. Embassy is aware of a significant increase in the number of COVID-19 cases since January 2021. The practice of COVID-19 mitigation and prevention measures remains limited. The Tanzanian government has not released aggregate numbers on COVID-19 cases or deaths since April 2020. Healthcare facilities in Tanzania can become quickly overwhelmed in a healthcare crisis,” it said in a travel advisory last week.

But Tanzanian authorities continue to deny this, indicating that while some cases have been reported, they are those of foreigners arriving in the country.

It is a situation such as this that makes Kenyans realise just why the elaborate measures outlined and enforced by the government as soon as the disease was declared a global health emergency were a wise and timely intervention.

While some of the effects such as business shutdowns, staff furloughs and high cost of living, among others, have made life unbearable for millions of Kenyans, the fact that the country is on course to overcoming the crisis is something to thank God and the government of President Uhuru Kenyatta for.

Had Uhuru not quickly moved to impose overnight curfews, cessation of movement to and from specific hotspots, social distancing in public places and wearing of masks, it is unlikely we that could today be boasting of a positive rate of less than three percent, which is below the five percent provided by the WHO as one of the indicators that a country is flattening the curve.

However, Kenyans must not drop the guard if the country is to return to normalcy, a situation that would enable the government to fully reopen the economy and allow Kenyans to return to their normal lives.
