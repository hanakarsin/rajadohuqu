Kendu Bay: A Strategically Situated, Fast-Growing Lakeside Town with No Banking Services

The banking system operates as the heart and lifeblood of any thriving economy. It is the key to economic evolution and development.

Banking is vital to unlocking wealth, providing jobs, creating opportunities, and facilitating commerce.

The presence of mainstream banking in an area spurs the residents' financial literacy, culminating in a strong and stable economy.

A town without a bank

Sadly, Kendu Bay town, which has been one of Homa Bay county’s commercial hubs for the longest time, grapples with ineffective banking.

The town is strategically located between major towns, including Oyugis, Homa Bay Town, and Katito. Within Homa Bay county it is linked with major roads.

According to the Kenya National Bureau of Statistics, the town’s population has been rising over the last ten years by approximately 14.8%. Currently, the total population in the town amounts to 6,064 persons.

The ancient town is situated along the shoreline of Lake Victoria hence making the town an ideal centre for banking operation due to the thriving fishing industry and other economic activities.

Being a sub-county headquarters, the town hosts civil servants, security officers, teachers and workers of non-governmental institutions, and business people.

The old town

The 2019 census report shows that about 178 688 persons in the Rachuonyo North sub-county are working. This means 43.9% of the area’s population are employed in either government or private sector.

The working class normally relies on banks to process salaries, send money and make savings. Due to such circumstances, Kendu town is proving to be in dire need of a mainstream bank.

The town is littered with palatial accommodation facilities, which is an indication that the town is doing pretty good economically.

During the colonial period, business activities were lively as Christian Missionaries, Arabs, and Indians domiciled in the rural town.

Kendu Bays old town is memorable for Arab traders who owned hundreds of shops better known as ‘Dukas’, which escalated business in the town.

The town's shoreline boasts of a pier constructed decades ago to enable vessels to dock, thus connecting to the port of Kisumu and other lakeside towns.

Traders and residents of Kendu Bay town are currently lobbying for financial institutions and the government to aid in establishing banks to spur the local economy.

Rosemary Adhiambo, who runs a major retail shop in the town, said the panic over security at times as they have to accumulate bulk cash and risk transporting them to banks 30 kilometres away in Homa Bay town.

She said on many occasions, and they opt to keep money in their homes which they find very risky for business.

"Sometimes we are even forced to keep money in the houses which are very dangerous, just because we don't have a bank," she said.

The trader said she is hopeful that mainstream banking will help ease business transactions, especially in this era of technology.

Jactone Otieno, on his part, said small and medium enterprises operators complain that lack of banking services has incapacitated the sustainability of their cooperative societies.

"Businesses in Kendu bay Town find it difficult to scale up because they cannot access loans from the bank to help them boost their business," Jactone said.

However, Otieno said some banks are providing alternative methods such as mobile phone banking and internet banking in an attempt to bridge the divide.

Kindu Bay has all that is required to sustain a bank

A Statistics Officer, Jared Otieno, on his account, noted that the town is one of the fastest-growing towns in the county.

The Officer said the town has all the necessities required to sustain a bank, and what is remaining is an initiative of the government and financial institution to set up a bank in the legendary town.

He stated that parents paying school fees to their children are compelled to flock to banks in the surrounding towns during the opening of schools.

Institutions in the town and its environment, including Kendu Sub County Hospital, Gendia Adventist Mission Hospital, polytechnics, and vocational training centres like Mawego Technical Training Institute, plus numerous primary and secondary schools, are all inconvenienced by the lack of a bank in the town.

The town is also endowed with major tourist destinations, including the lake Simbi Nyaima and Ondago Swamp, which, if managed properly, would spur the local economy.

It is also famous as the epitome of Nyanza entertainment, with its renowned 'Kendu Show', which attract musicians and bands from all over the country.

Serene Kotieno beach in the town attracts numerous local and other tourists. In addition, the beach acts as a resort bay for those who use Lake Victoria for transport and traders who visit the region.

Fishing in the lake generates a lot of income for the town, making the town vibrant and appropriate for a banking. Moreover, agriculture is practised extensively in the area, which contributes immensely to the town's economy.

Kendu Bay town also prides itself as the home of former US president Brack Obama’s father.
